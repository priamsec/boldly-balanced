On January 1st, I set up a spreadsheet. Sleep latency, HRV, VO2 max, grip strength, reaction time, mood scores, cognitive benchmark tests, skin conductance, daily step counts. I was going to optimize my way to a better version of myself, one data point at a time. The premise felt scientific. The results were humbling.

Ninety days later, I had approximately 2,700 hours of tracked data, a spreadsheet that would make any quantified-self enthusiast envious, and some genuinely surprising findings about what actually moves the needle on feeling and performing better. Not the latest supplement stack. Not the most expensive wearable. Not the most aggressive fasting protocol. The basics, applied consistently.

The Context: Who This Is For

Before diving in, I want to be clear about the constraints. I'm a generally healthy male in my mid-30s. I was not starting from a place of illness, burnout, or metabolic crisis. I was optimizing for energy, cognitive performance, and physical capability. That's an important context because many biohacking claims come from people with specific health problems chasing specific solutions — a population where even marginal improvements can be transformative.

If you're dealing with a diagnosed condition, this account is not medical advice. Work with a practitioner who can interpret your specific labs and symptoms. But if you're healthy, functional, and curious about what a systematic optimization approach actually delivers, read on.

Baseline Metrics and the ThreePhase Structure

My baseline metrics at day one: 7 hours 15 minutes average sleep, HRV of 62ms measured via chest strap, VO2 max of 44 ml/kg/min placing me above average for my age group, resting heart rate of 58bpm, and a Stroop test score placing me in the 65th percentile for cognitive performance in my demographic.

I divided the 90 days into three phases deliberately:

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Foundation — sleep optimization, cold exposure, baseline tracking
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Enhancement — supplements, breathwork protocols, targeted exercise
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Integration — consolidate what worked, eliminate what didn't

The sequencing mattered. Establish the foundation first. Then layer enhancements. Then ruthlessly eliminate what doesn't earn its place in your routine.

Phase 1 Results: Sleep Was the Whole Game

Phase 1 interventions were deliberately low-stakes. The only additions to my routine: consistent bedtime within a 15-minute window every night, no food in the final 3 hours before sleep, and a 90-second cold shower each morning.

After 30 days on this minimal protocol, my average HRV increased from 62 to 71ms — a 14% improvement that showed up consistently across all measurements. Sleep latency dropped from 18 minutes to 9 minutes. Subjectively, I felt noticeably more alert in the morning and reported fewer afternoon energy crashes.

The cold shower was initially aversive but became routine by day 12. By week three, it felt merely uncomfortable rather than punishing. The mechanism is well-documented: cold exposure activates the sympathetic nervous system, followed by a compensatory parasympathetic rebound that leaves you feeling calmer and more alert than before the exposure. Think of it as a daily free reboot of your stress response system.

What surprised me most was how rapidly sleep improvements showed up in the metrics. Within two weeks of fixing bedtime consistency, my sleep efficiency jumped from 84% to 89%. The body responds quickly when you remove the disruptors.

Phase 2: The Supplement Stack Gets Expensive and Disappointing

Phase 2 was where I got enthusiastic — and where the data started telling a different story than my enthusiasm.

Based on a review of the literature and consultation with a functional medicine practitioner, I added a supplement protocol targeting cognitive performance:

  • Creatine monohydrate: 5g daily for cognitive support through ATP regeneration in the brain
  • L-theanine + caffeine: 200mg + 100mg morning dose for focus without the jitters
  • Omega-3 fish oil: 2g EPA/DHA combined for anti-inflammatory effects
  • Vitamin D: 4,000 IU daily (I tested deficient at baseline)
  • NMN: 500mg daily for NAD+ precursor support and cellular energy metabolism

After 30 days on this stack, the results were underwhelming. Creatine showed a marginal improvement in complex task performance — a 4% gain on working memory tasks, consistent across multiple trials. The L-theanine/caffeine combination was noticeably effective for focus but performed no better than what I got from proper sleep alone. The other supplements produced no measurable changes in any tracked metric.

This was the first major surprise: the supplements with the most robust evidence behind them showed small effects. The more speculative interventions — NMN, high-dose omega-3 in a non-inflammatory state — showed nothing measurable in someone starting from a baseline of adequate nutrition. The dose-response curve for most supplements in healthy people is nearly flat.

Phase 3: What Actually Mattered

By phase 3, I had eliminated the NMN, reduced omega-3 to a maintenance dose, and focused on what had produced measurable changes:

  • Cold exposure: Daily cold shower maintained the HRV gains
  • Sleep consistency: The single highest-impact intervention, confirmed across all three months
  • Creatine: Small but consistent cognitive benefit, continued
  • L-theanine + caffeine: Useful tactical tool for high-demand days, not a daily requirement

I also added a 10-minute daily meditation practice in the final three weeks — not part of the original plan, but the data showed that days following poor sleep but with a meditation session were measurably better on cognitive metrics than days following poor sleep without one. Meditation appeared to partially compensate for sleep debt. The effect was small but consistent.

The Final Numbers: What 90 Days Actually Delivered

After 90 days, comparing month 3 to month 1 baselines:

  • HRV: 62 → 74ms (+19%)
  • Sleep efficiency: 84% → 91%
  • VO2 max: 44 → 47 ml/kg/min (+7%)
  • Cognitive composite: 65th percentile → 72nd percentile
  • Resting heart rate: 58 → 54 bpm

Not transformative, but meaningful. The key finding: all of these gains came from three interventions applied consistently. Better sleep. Daily cold exposure. Brief meditation. The expensive supplements and advanced diagnostics contributed almost nothing for someone starting from a baseline of good health.

What This Means for Your Own Optimization

If you're healthy and looking to optimize, the evidence strongly suggests the following hierarchy of interventions. Start at the top. Only move down when you've genuinely maxed out the previous tier.

Start with sleep. Consistent, adequate sleep — 7-9 hours, regular timing, cool environment, no food close to bedtime — is the highest-leverage intervention available. No supplement comes close to matching its effects on every metric that matters: cognition, mood, physical performance, immune function, and longevity. The research on sleep extension and cognitive performance is robust and reproducible. If you do nothing else, do this.

Add cold exposure. A daily cold shower or cold plunge is cheap, takes 2-3 minutes, and produces measurable improvements in HRV, mood, and alertness. The adaptogenic effect is real and sustainable in a way that stimulant-based interventions are not. Start with 30 seconds and build up over weeks.

Consider creatine and L-theanine as tactical tools. These are the two supplements with the strongest evidence for cognitive enhancement in healthy individuals. Creatine works through energy metabolism in the brain. L-theanine works through modulating alpha brain wave activity. Both are well-tolerated, inexpensive, and measurably effective. Neither requires a prescription or a functional medicine consult.

Be skeptical of everything else. NAD+ precursors, extreme-dose omega-3s, nootropics stacks, genetic reports that promise personalized protocols — the evidence for these interventions in healthy people ranges from thin to nonexistent. The biohacking industry's most enthusiastic claims target people who are already optimizing and looking for marginal gains at high cost. The last 5% is exponentially more expensive than the first 95%.

The most profound finding from 90 days of self-experimentation was how little I needed to add to live noticeably better. The foundation — sleep, cold, breath — is unsexy but effective. Everything else is marginal. Optimize the fundamentals first. Then decide whether the remaining distance is worth the effort and expense.
